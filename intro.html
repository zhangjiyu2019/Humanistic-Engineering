A Framework for Ontological Engineering

On the transformation of the problem of suffering into a mathematical problem

First principles demand stripping away all prior assumptions and rebuilding from the most foundational axiom. In engineering this means defining requirements before seeking solutions within physical constraints. In philosophy its counterpart is phenomenological reduction — suspending all ready-made judgements and returning to the first-person experiential datum itself.

Pushed to its limit, this reduction converges across cultures and centuries on a single phenomenon: suffering is a structural feature of human existence. Buddhist thought names it the First Noble Truth. Ancient Greek pessimism voices it through the Silenic answer. The cross-cultural, cross-historical consistency of this convergence suggests suffering is not an emotional construction — it is an objective description of how the human organism operates under specific physical and cognitive conditions.

The Core Proposition

Humanistic Engineering treats the ultimate problem of human existence — the problem of suffering — as a definable, solvable engineering problem. Its central proposition is that this problem can, in principle, be transformed into a mathematical problem.

Philosophical work establishes suffering as a precisely defined problem. Scientific and engineering work converges it toward a solution. The hinge between them is the transformation from an existential question to a mathematical one: what can be precisely defined can be mathematised; what can be mathematised can be optimised.

The transformation is made possible by two conceptual pivots. Internally: when neuroscience reaches sufficient resolution, "suffering" and "flourishing" cease to be subjective vocabulary and become geometric forms in high-dimensional space — computable neural topologies that constitute a loss function. Externally: once that objective function is established, all human practical activity collapses into engineering causal variables, and the problem becomes one of pure differentiation and optimisation.

The Structural Limit of Prior Approaches

The history of responses to suffering — Buddhism, Stoicism, existentialism, depth psychology, pharmacology — represents the best attempts possible under the constraints of each era. Their shared structural limit is not a failure of insight but a failure of execution: the terminal of every solution is always the human itself.

Humans are part of the problem. No purely intellectual effort can rewrite a physiological mechanism from within it. No single practitioner can scale. No tradition possesses an independent verification standard for its terminal state. This forms a self-enclosed loop that has never been broken — not because the problem is insoluble, but because the computational tool capable of operating outside the loop did not exist. Until now.

The Variable That Changes the Equation

AI does not constitute the core of this framework. It is the tool that moves the lever, not the lever itself. But it is, for the first time in history, a tool structurally capable of breaking the self-enclosed loop — a large-scale information processing system isomorphic with the human brain, yet infinitely scalable and accelerable beyond human cognitive constraints.

The correct mode of instruction follows the AlphaZero model: provide AI not with pre-concluded moral answers, but with the rules of the game — the thermodynamic structure of physical reality and the predictive coding mechanics of consciousness. From those rules, the optimal solution is derived autonomously, uncontaminated by the historical limits of human moral systems.

What remains irreducibly human — the only input that cannot be delegated — is the intentional vector itself: the directional impulse that marks one state of the world as "I want this" and another as "I do not want that." Without a subject to establish that vector, the universe has no value axis. Humanity's non-transferable role is to provide the first push. The rest is derivation.

Optimistic Pessimism

The position sustaining this work is one of optimistic pessimism: the reality of suffering at the level of deep architecture must be acknowledged without flinching. And yet the current convergence of technical variables makes its ultimate solvability a cautious, reasoned engineering judgment — not an emotion.

This is not technological accelerationism. Accelerationism concerns itself with speed; this concerns itself with direction. As long as the vector of human intentionality is transmitted with sufficient fidelity and the object of inquiry is defined with sufficient precision, the millennia-long march against suffering may be completed in the instant of convergence. Give me a lever, and I will move the Earth. The lever is the alignment of the ultimate existential problem with the ultimate technical variable on the same coordinate system. The task is to find the fulcrum.
